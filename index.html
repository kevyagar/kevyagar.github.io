What are Counting Numbers?

Counting, Combinatorics, Real World Applications/Uses

Counting numbers sounds like exactly what it's about, counting numbers. We use combinatorics to help us with counting, and this can be referred to as "enumerative combinatorics."

Combinatorics are useful when we try to figure out the number of possible outcomes based on the number of decisions.

Perhaps best used today by social media to find connections with people we do not directly know, like for example Instagram. You have followers, and those followers have their own followers, and those followers have their own followers and so on. Combinatorics in this case is used to help you find people who you can follow based off various things, such as mutual followers.

Combinatorics are also used in probability to discover the chance of getting a desired outcome.

Combinatorics

Combinatorics: The branch of mathematics dealing with combinations of objects belonging to a finite set in accordance with certain constraints, such as those of graph theory.

Addition Rule

Question: You want to buy a cell phone, the local verizon store has three iPhone 11’s on sale (black, white, purple) and two Samsung Galaxy 10’s on sale (black, white). How many possible ways are there to pick a phone?

Solution: You can purchase either an iPhone 11 or a Samsung Galaxy 10. Since there are three ways to pick an iPhone and two ways to pick a Samsung Galaxy 10 there are five ways to pick a phone (3+2=5)

Addition Rule: If we have two choices C1 and C2, with C1 having possible outcomes O1 and C2 having possible outcomes O2, with |O1| =n1 and |O2| =n2, then if the sets O1 and O2 are disjoint (non-overlapping) the total number of outcomes for C1 or C2 having n1,..., nk possible (non-overlapping) outcomes, then the total number of ways of C1 occurring or C2 occuring or Ck occuring is n1+n2+.....+nk.

In combinatorics, the addition rule is stated simply as the idea that if we have A ways of doing something and B ways of doing another thing and we can not do both at the same time, then there are A + B ways to choose one of the actions.

Multiplcation Rule

Question: There are three types of shoes: boots, sneakers, and dress shoes. Each shoe comes in two colors, black or brown. How many possible shoe configurations are there?

Solution: First we must make our first choice, C1, which is the type of shoe. Then our second choice, C2, is what color of shoe. The outcome of C1 contains four possibilities, |O1| = 3, and the outcome of C2 contains 2 possibilities, |O2| = 2. Now we apply the multiplication rule: Total number of outcomes = |O1| x |O2| = 3 x 2 = 6. Therefore there are 6 total shoe configurations.

Multiplication Rule: If we have two choices C1 and C2, with C1 having possible outcomes O1 and C2 having possible outcomes O2, with |O1| = n1 and |O2| = n2, then the total number of possible outcomes associated with C1 and C2 occurring is n1 x n. More generally, if we have k choices C1, … , Ck having n1, … , nk possible outcomes, then the total number of ways of C1 occurring and C2 occurring and … and Ck occurring is n1 x n2 x … x nk.

In combinatorics, the multiplication rule is stated simply as the idea that if there are a ways of doing something and b ways of doing another thing, then there are a x b ways of performing both actions.

Permutation

Question: You want to place 4 students in 4 seats. How many different ways can their seats be arranged?

Solution: There are 4 students, there's 4 kids who can sit in the first seat. After, there are now 3 students to pick from for the second seat, once a student is selected for the second seat, then now there is only two students left for the third seat and after that seating is done, there's one student left for the last seat. There are 24 ways of seating the students, 4 x 3 x 2 x 1 = 24.

Permutation: A permutation of objects is an arrangement of those objects where order does matter. For example a pass code of 1234 is different from a pass code of 4321. Even though both codes contain the same numbers (1234) they are not the same because the order of those numbers are different. Also, each object can only be used once in the permutation.

The formula for calculating the number of permutations for r items chosen from a total of n items is denoted P(n, r). So the number of ways to permute four students into four chairs would be P(4, 4). The formula for calculating P(n, r) is given above, by plugging in n as 4 and r as 4, you will get 24.

Combination

Solution: You can hangout with Bob and Charlie, which is the same as Charlie and Bob. In this case, you can list out every possible combination of friends and remove the duplicate, or you can use the formula.
